Math Power-Up: Lesson 1

Let's explore the secrets hidden in numbers!

1. Number Patterns (Arithmetic Sequences)

A **Number Pattern** is just a list of numbers (called **terms**) that follows a rule. For our lesson, we are focusing on the most common type: the **Arithmetic Sequence**.

The Constant Jump: Common Difference (d)

In an arithmetic sequence, you always add (or subtract) the same number to get from one term to the next. This constant value is called the **Common Difference ($d$)**.

Example 1: 5, 8, 11, 14, 17, ...

To get from 5 to 8, you add 3. To get from 8 to 11, you add 3. The common difference $d$ is 3.

Example 2: 20, 16, 12, 8, ...

To get from 20 to 16, you add -4 (or subtract 4). The common difference $d$ is -4.

The Magic Formula: The General Term ($T_n$)

If you want to find the 50th term or the 834th term, you don't want to count all the way! We use a single formula, the **General Term ($T_n$)**, to find *any* term based on its position ($n$).

Tn = a + (n-1)d

  • Tn: The value of the nth term you are looking for.
  • a: The first term (T1).
  • n: The term number (1st, 2nd, 3rd, etc.).
  • d: The common difference.

Deep Dive: Developing the General Term (The Hard Part!)

This is where you go from a list of numbers to the formula!

Sequence: 5, 11, 17, 23, ...

Step 1: Find the Common Difference ($d$).

11 - 5 = 6. So, $d = 6$.

Step 2: Compare the terms to the multiples of $d$ (which is 6).

Multiples of 6: 6, 12, 18, 24, ...

Our Sequence: 5, 11, 17, 23, ...

Step 3: Find the link. How do you get from the multiple of 6 to the term in the sequence?

6 - 1 = 5 (1st term)

12 - 1 = 11 (2nd term)

18 - 1 = 17 (3rd term)

Step 4: Write the General Term ($T_n$)

Since the nth multiple of 6 is 6n, and we subtract 1 each time, the formula is:

Tn = 6n - 1

2. Binary Numbers (Base Two)

You use the **Decimal System** (Base 10) every day. It uses 10 digits (0-9) and place values that are powers of 10 (100, 101, 102, $\dots$). Computers don't have 10 fingers, they only have two states: **ON** (1) and **OFF** (0). This is the **Binary System** (Base 2).

The Binary Place Values

In Base 2, the place values are powers of 2.

... 25=32

24=16

23=8

22=4

21=2

20=1

Converting Binary to Decimal (The Easy Way!)

Just multiply the binary digit by its place value and add them up.

Example: Convert 1101two to Decimal

1101two = (1 × 23) + (1 × 22) + (0 × 21) + (1 × 20)

= (1 × 8) + (1 × 4) + (0 × 2) + (1 × 1)

= 8 + 4 + 0 + 1 = 13

So, 1101two = 13ten.

Complex Concept: Converting Decimal to Binary (The Division Method)

This is the most reliable way for large numbers: continuously divide the decimal number by 2 and keep track of the remainders. **The binary number is read from the last remainder up to the first!**

Example: Convert 22ten to Binary

22 ÷ 2 = 11, Remainder **0**

11 ÷ 2 = 5, Remainder **1**

5 ÷ 2 = 2, Remainder **1**

2 ÷ 2 = 1, Remainder **0**

1 ÷ 2 = 0, Remainder **1**

Read up: 10110two

Binary Addition (The Rules are Different!)

The only rule you need to remember is: **1 + 1 = 10two** (which means 0, carry the 1).

Example: 101two + 11two

   1  1  (Carry)
  1 0 1
+   1 1
-----
 1 0 0 0

1. Right Column: 1 + 1 = 0 (carry 1).

2. Middle Column: 0 + 1 + 1 (carry) = 0 (carry 1).

3. Left Column: 1 + 0 + 1 (carry) = 0 (carry 1).

4. New Column: 0 + 0 + 1 (carry) = 1.

Answer: 1000two (Decimal 8)
